## Starting behind the stage

Across fifteen campus productions, I moved between volunteer, stage manager and control roles. Beyond the spotlight, every performance depended on an invisible high-pressure system: countdowns, headset cues, fluorescent tape and people constantly correcting human error.

> The audience sees magic. The crew lives inside the countdown.

The project returns the builders to the center. Rather than recreating a show, it spatializes backstage order, bodily pressure and the condition of being present through absence.

## Three positions of seeing

The staff weave chaos into a grid; actors move between symbolic radiance and physical vulnerability; the audience completes the dream by agreeing to believe. They share one stage while inhabiting different systems of time.

## Marks in the dark

T, L and X tape marks are more than coordinates. They hold memories of waiting, weight, emergency and movement. Visitors scan the marks to uncover radio static, footsteps and fragmented instructions.

<MediaFigure src={marks} alt="Fluorescent stage marks in a dark space" caption="Stage marks become an index into memory" />

## A luminous interface

The translucent kinetic object links the physical room with the VR layer. It resembles both a timing machine and a compressed stage skeleton: approaching it gradually exposes labor that darkness normally protects.

## Reflection

Invisibility is not absence. It is an order of attention produced by design. The next iteration will incorporate more voices and movement traces from backstage workers, expanding a personal memory into a shared archive.
